What is OBV?

Originally developed by Joseph Granville in the 1960s, On-Balance Volume (OBV) is a momentum indicator that ties volume to price direction. It operates under a foundational belief in technical analysis:

volume precedes price.

Here's the basic idea:

If today‚Äôs close is higher than yesterday‚Äôs, the day‚Äôs volume is added to the OBV.

If today‚Äôs close is lower, the volume is subtracted.

If there is no price change, OBV remains the same.

Granville described OBV as the "lead dog" of market action, pulling price behind it. The cumulative total offers a running snapshot of demand and supply dynamics, making it easier to identify whether the current price movement is truly supported by investor participation.

While OBV doesn‚Äôt tell you exact levels to buy or sell, its direction and patterns often foreshadow major price moves.

How OBV is Calculated

The math behind OBV is refreshingly simple:

OBV (today) = OBV (yesterday) + volume (if price is up)

OBV (today) = OBV (yesterday) - volume (if price is down)

OBV (today) = OBV (yesterday) (if price is unchanged)

Because OBV is cumulative, it smooths out short-term noise and presents a clearer view of long-term volume flow trends. It is also particularly useful in identifying stealth accumulation or distribution that may not yet be evident in price action.

Why OBV Matters: Key Interpretations

1. Trend Confirmation

If the OBV and price are both rising, the trend has strong support. Same applies in reverse. This helps traders avoid false breakouts or premature reversals by ensuring there's genuine interest behind a move.

Rising price + rising OBV = strong bullish trend

Falling price + falling OBV = confirmed downtrend

This use of OBV as a confirmation tool is especially helpful in trending markets, where traders want to ride the momentum but need reassurance that it‚Äôs not built on shaky ground.

2. Non-Confirmation (Early Warnings)

When price hits a new high but OBV doesn‚Äôt, something‚Äôs off. This often means that volume isn‚Äôt keeping up, and a reversal may be looming. Traders use this as a cue to either reduce position size or trail stop losses more aggressively.

Price up, OBV flat/down = weakening uptrend

Price down, OBV flat/up = weakening downtrend

These subtle clues can help traders anticipate changes before they become obvious to the broader market.

3. Divergence Signals (Reversals)

Bullish Divergence

: Price hits new lows, but OBV makes higher lows ‚Üí Potential bottom. Often an early sign of institutional accumulation.

Bearish Divergence

: Price hits new highs, but OBV makes lower highs ‚Üí Potential top. Suggests distribution behind the scenes.

Divergence trading is one of the more advanced applications of OBV. While it requires patience and discipline, it can offer high reward-to-risk trade setups, especially when paired with candlestick signals or chart patterns like double bottoms or head and shoulders.

üí∞ Crypto

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Ignoring Price Action

: OBV is best used

Overtrading Divergences

: Not all divergences lead to reversals. Wait for confirmation.

Forgetting News Events

: Sudden volume spikes can distort OBV. Filter out events like earnings or CPI days.

As with all indicators, context is key. Use OBV to enhance, not replace, your broader trading process.
